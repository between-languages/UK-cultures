A Liverpudlian and a Somerset local may genuinely struggle to understand each other.

Module 2 · Social Customs & Etiquette
How the four nations interact — and what really lies beneath the surface.
01

English social culture is famously indirect — 'quite interesting' often means 'I disagree'.

02

'Not bad' is high praise; 'You might want to consider…' means 'please change this'.

03

Politeness is expressed through restraint, queuing and never causing a scene.

04

The English are not cold — warmth is real but shown through understatement, not effusiveness.

Scots dislike being labelled 'British' over 'Scottish' — national identity matters.

Key Terms & Phrases
Words that don't translate — and what they actually mean.
Scouse
England
The distinctive accent of Liverpool. Named after 'lobscouse', a sailor's stew popular in the port city.
Geordie
England
The accent and dialect of Newcastle upon Tyne. One of the most recognisable dialects in England.
Cockney
England
East London's working-class dialect, famous for rhyming slang. Spoken by those born near Bow Bells church.
RP (Received Pronunciation)
England
The 'BBC accent' — spoken natively by fewer than 3% of English people. Prestigious but rare.
Not bad
England
High praise in English understatement. If someone says your work is 'not bad', you've done very well.
Quite interesting
England
Polite English disagreement. Often means 'I have serious doubts about this'. Classic understatement.
Wee
Scotland
Small or little. A core Scots vocabulary word used in everyday speech, not just for effect.
Braw
Scotland
Fine, good, attractive. From Scots. "A braw day" = a fine day.
Aye
Scotland
Yes. Used in everyday conversation across Scotland and in Ulster-Scots speech in Northern Ireland.
Wenglish
Wales
The natural mixing of Welsh and English mid-sentence — code-switching for bilingual Welsh speakers.
Hwyl
Wales
An untranslatable concept: passion, inspiration and communal fervour. Felt at rugby matches and choral events.
Senedd
Wales
The Welsh Parliament (pronounced 'SEH-neth'). Has powers over health, education and other devolved matters.
Lilt
Wales
The melodic, sing-song quality of Welsh-accented English, inherited from Welsh language stress patterns.
What about ye?
Northern Ireland
Standard Belfast greeting meaning 'How are you?'. Not aggressive — a genuinely warm opener.
Ulster-Scots
Northern Ireland
A dialect and recognised minority language of Scots spoken in parts of Northern Ireland.
The Troubles
Northern Ireland
The ethno-nationalist conflict in Northern Ireland (1968–1998). A sensitive topic requiring care.
